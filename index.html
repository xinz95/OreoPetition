INCIDENT #1 Reese's Cups defeat Oreos in the Snacket bracket

In the most puzzling outcome of the 2026 Snacket bracket, Reese's Peanut Butter Cups edged out the Oreo in a closer-than-it-should-have-been vote. The bracket advanced. The cookie did not. And a lot of people had questions.

To be clear: Reese's are good. Nobody is disputing that. But beating the Oreo? A cookie with over a century of history, a dedicated dunking tradition, and more flavor varieties than most candy aisles? That outcome deserves some scrutiny.

Also: while we're here
INCIDENT #2 Cheez-Its defeat Goldfish. Yes, really.

The Oreo wasn't the only beloved snack shown the door in 2026. Goldfish crackers — a snack so universally loved it's practically a food group — were knocked out by Cheez-Its. Now, look. Cheez-Its are legitimately good. This is not a Cheez-It hit piece. But Goldfish are an institution.

They come in a bag shaped like a fish. They've been in every kid's lunchbox since 1962. They're cheddar but somehow not too cheddar. How does a snack this charming lose to a cracker that's basically just a square? The shape alone should have carried Goldfish through at least two more rounds.

Our position

Two great snacks. Both eliminated too early. The bracket owes us answers.

We have nothing against Reese's or Cheez-Its. They are perfectly fine treats. But "perfectly fine" and "better than an Oreo" or "better than Goldfish" are two very different things, and this bracket confused them — twice.

We're asking for one simple acknowledgment: that the 2026 Snacket bracket made some questionable calls, and the snacking public deserves better seeding methodology going forward.

The formal grievances
  • I
    Dunkability was not part of the scoring criteriaA major oversight. Any snack bracket that doesn't weight dunkability is working with an incomplete rubric.
  • II
    The Double Stuf wasn't given its own seedIt is arguably a different and superior product. Lumping it in with the classic Oreo may have diluted the vote.
  • III
    Cultural legacy was underweighted across the boardOreos since 1912. Goldfish since 1962. Combined, that's over 200 years of snack history dismissed in two matchups.
  • IV
    No rematch clause existsA truly fair bracket would have a mechanism for contesting obviously surprising results. We are proposing one now.
